The new weekly Socialist Worker

October 12, 2001 | Page 13

ANYONE WHO has attended a protest or a picket knows what it's like to open the newspaper and find your struggle misrepresented--or not reported at all.

That's just one reason why socialists and other radicals have always produced their own newspapers--to tell the story of struggles that the mainstream press won't.

But these publications also play an important role in helping activists to organize themselves and bring others into the struggle.

During the great Minneapolis Teamsters Rebellion of 1934, strike leaders--the core of them socialists--put together a daily strike newspaper called The Organizer. The paper "enabled the union to break the capitalist monopoly on the press, thereby cutting through the propaganda screen erected by the bosses," wrote Farrell Dobbs.

"The strikers and their sympathizers got an accurate account each day of key developments during the previous 24 hours…The whole labor movement was alerted against dangers that arose, and the way to cope with them was carefully explained. Although only a two-page tabloid, the daily Organizer packed a wallop, and the working class of the city soon came to swear by it."

In the same way, Socialist Worker counters the lies of the bosses' press and gives a platform for our side--from union activists to death row prisoners fighting for justice. We take up discussions about the next step in our struggles and the political debates within our movement.

Ultimately, SW aims to show how the horrors of the society we live in have a root in capitalism--and that the struggles against those horrors can be linked. A fair, just and democratic world is possible--but we have to fight for it.

With this issue, SW has gone weekly--with the goal of strengthening our struggle for a better world.
